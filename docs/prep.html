9 Tell me about a time you turned complex analysis into an actionable recommendation.

  • Working with Thames reach for dissertation

S

In my dissertation, I was investigating how built-environment features and perceptions of safety were linked to rough sleeping patterns in London. The project involved combining person-level referral data with street-level image data and spatial information, which made the analysis quite complex. The challenge was that there were lots of possible variables and patterns, the end goal was to generate insights that could actually be useful for policy and intervention with charities such as thamesreach.

T

My task was to analyse this complex, multi-source dataset and identify which environmental factors were most strongly associated with rough sleeping locations, then translate that into recommendations that would be understandable and relevant for a non-technical audience at Thames Reach

A

To do that, I brought together multiple datasets, including geospatial referral data and street-level image-based measures of the urban environment. I used methods like clustering to identify different types of rough sleeping locations, spatial autocorrelation measures such as Moran’s I to understand patterns in the data, and random forest models to explore which built-environment and perceived-safety variables were most predictive.

Because the outputs were quite technical and there were ethical sensitivities around oversimplifying homelessness. I focused on identifying the main patterns, checking that the findings were robust, and then translating them into clear messages about which environmental characteristics were associated with higher concentrations of rough sleeping and assessing this throughout current policy.

R

The outcome was that I was able to turn a complex and highly multidimensional analysis into a practical recommendation: that interventions should be more spatially targeted, and that local authorities and support services could use environmental and perceptual indicators to help prioritise areas for outreach and prevention.

I scored a distinction. (yay)

18 The role mentions statistical modelling. What does that mean to you in practice?


Statistical modelling in practice is about using data in a structured way to understand performance, identify patterns, and support decisions. It can include more advanced predictive techniques, but in many operational roles it also means applying methods like trend analysis, KPI monitoring, and anomaly detection. Trend analysis is about looking at how a metric changes over time to understand whether performance is stable, improving, or deteriorating. KPI monitoring is about tracking the most important measures against targets, thresholds, or expected ranges so it is clear where performance is on track and where it may need attention. Anomaly detection is about spotting values or patterns that fall outside what would normally be expected, which can help flag possible operational issues, emerging risk, or data quality problems. So overall, I’d describe statistical modelling as using data systematically to move beyond simple reporting and help identify where further investigation or action may be needed.

19 Can you explain a time when you had to analyse a complex set of data and make recommendations based on your findings?

S

For my MSc dissertation at UCL, I focused on homeless rough sleepers and displacement across London boroughs. The project involved working with sensitive geospatial referral data gathered through collaboration with Thames Reach and the StreetLink application. It was a complex problem because I wasn’t just analysing one dataset I was trying to understand a real-world social issue by combining spatial data, environmental context, and policy considerations around homelessness and displacement

T

My task was to build a robust analytical approach that could identify patterns in rough sleeper movement and the environments associated with displacement, while making sure the work was ethically sound, anonymised, and useful for stakeholders. I also needed to go beyond pure modelling and think about the policy relevance of the findings, in other words, what the results meant for homelessness outreach and decision-making.

A

I started by carefully preparing and structuring the referral and geospatial data, making sure anonymity and data security were maintained throughout. I then used Python, including tools such as pandas, numpy and scikit-learn, to build analytical models that combined geospatial analysis with computer vision methods. The aim was to link features of the built environment to possible migration or displacement patterns among rough sleepers.

Alongside the modelling, I interpreted the findings in a wider policy context. I looked at what the patterns might suggest about displacement, how environments may influence movement, and how those insights could support outreach services. So it wasn’t just a technical exercise it was also about translating the outputs into something meaningful and responsible for real-world use.

I then presented the findings clearly to homeless outreach workers, focusing on the practical implications and making sure the recommendations were understandable to non-technical stakeholders.

R

The result was a robust and novel model that helped predict the flow of rough sleepers in cases of displacement, while maintaining strong data anonymity and security. More importantly, the work gave outreach workers a better understanding of the kinds of environments rough sleepers may migrate through when displaced, which made the findings practically useful as well as analytically strong. I think that project showed that I can handle complex data, build models, and also connect the findings back to policy and stakeholder needs.

28 What techniques do you use in qualitative data analysis?

The main techniques I use are thematic analysis, reviewing stakeholder feedback for repeated issues or patterns, comparing views across different groups, and summarising unstructured information into clear themes. I also use qualitative insight to support quantitative analysis, for example to understand why a trend may be happening rather than just reporting that it exists. I think qualitative analysis is most useful when it helps connect the numbers to real operational behaviour, stakeholder priorities, or data collection issues. My focus is usually on being structured and consistent, so that the findings are still evidence-based rather than just anecdotal.

29 How do you prioritize your analysis when dealing with multiple projects?

I usually prioritise based on business impact, urgency, and dependency. First, I look at which pieces of work are most critical to operational decisions or external reporting deadlines. Then I consider whether one task is blocking another, for example if a dataset needs cleaning before reporting can happen. I also try to distinguish between work that is urgent and work that is important, because they are not always the same. In practice, I like to break work into clear stages, such as data collection, cleaning, analysis, validation, and output, so I can track progress and adjust if priorities shift. Communication is also important. If I am working across multiple requests, I make sure stakeholders understand timelines and any trade-offs, so expectations stay realistic while quality remains high
